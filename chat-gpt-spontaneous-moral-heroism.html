There is a term for acts of instinctive heroism undertaken in situations one could never have foreseen happening before the moment that required it. The passengers who attempted to storm the cockpit on Flight 93 embody it. So did Liviu Librescu in barricading the door to protect his students. So also every soldier who jumped on a grenade to save his comrades. What is this term and what other examples do you know of that embody it?

The term you’re reaching for is most often “moral courage”, but in the very specific sense you’re describing—unpremeditated, instinctive self-sacrifice in an unforeseeable crisis—the phrase that fits best is:

“Spontaneous moral heroism”

(also sometimes called “situational heroism”)

This isn’t a single canonical word in everyday English, but it is a well-established concept in moral psychology and ethics.

Why this term fits your examples

What unites Flight 93, Liviu Librescu, and the soldier who falls on a grenade is that:

  • There was no prior plan or training for that exact moment

  • No time for deliberation

  • The act involved near-certain personal cost

  • The choice emerged instantly from character, not calculation

Researchers like Philip Zimbardo (of Stanford Prison Experiment fame) use situational heroism to describe acts that arise when ordinary people respond extraordinarily to sudden moral emergencies.

Zimbardo contrasts this with:

  • Planned heroism (firefighters, lifeguards, soldiers on patrol)

  • Role-based heroism (doing one’s duty)

  • Spontaneous heroism (no role, no rehearsal, no expectation)

Your examples fall squarely into the last category.

Closely related terms (and why they’re not quite right)

Moral courage

  • ✔ Emphasizes ethical resolve

  • ✘ Often involves deliberation and sustained opposition (e.g., whistleblowers)

Altruistic self-sacrifice

  • ✔ Describes the outcome precisely

  • ✘ Clinical, evolutionary, emotionally flat

Virtue ethics / Arete

  • ✔ Aristotle would say these acts reveal a formed character

  • ✘ Not a commonly used descriptive term for events

The “Good Samaritan”

  • ✔ Captures moral instinct

  • ✘ Usually non-lethal risk and somewhat foreseeable

Other powerful examples of spontaneous moral heroism

Civilian, sudden-crisis cases

  • Wesley Autrey, the “Subway Hero,” who jumped onto the tracks to shield a stranger from an oncoming train

  • Arland D. Williams Jr., Air Florida Flight 90 passenger, who repeatedly passed the rescue line to others before drowning

  • The bystanders at the 2017 London Bridge attack who charged armed attackers with chairs and fire extinguishers

Educational and protective acts

  • Aaron Feis and Chris Hixon, teachers at Marjory Stoneman Douglas High School, who ran toward gunfire

  • Sandy Hook staff members who shielded children with their bodies

Wartime and disaster contexts

  • Soldiers jumping on grenades (the paradigmatic case)

  • Mykhailo Dianov and others who held positions in Mariupol long after survival odds vanished

  • Nuclear plant workers at Chernobyl who entered lethal radiation zones to prevent wider catastrophe

Ancient and literary analogues

  • Titus Manlius’ soldiers in Roman histories who chose death to hold a line

  • Sydney Carton in A Tale of Two Cities (“It is a far, far better thing…”)

  • Beowulf’s retainer Wiglaf, the only one who stands with him against the dragon

Why this kind of heroism matters philosophically

These acts are unsettling because they:

  • Cannot be explained by incentives

  • Do not rely on identity or ideology

  • Occur faster than conscious reasoning

They suggest that:

Character is real, virtue can be habituated, and some moral truths live deeper than thought.

Or, as Hannah Arendt put it in a related context: in emergencies, people reveal who they are.

Is there a classical term for it? Ie from classical philosophy, Latin, Greek, or Classical Chinese.

Yes. There are classical terms for this kind of act—but none of them map perfectly one-to-one, because what you are isolating is a cross-cultural moral intuition that predates modern psychology but was framed differently in each tradition.

Below are the closest classical equivalents, ordered from most precise to most philosophically illuminating.

1. Greek: ἀνδρεία (andreía) — “manly courage” / moral courage

This is the central Greek term.

  • In Aristotle (Nicomachean Ethics, Book III), andreía is courage displayed in sudden danger, especially when:

    • There is no time for deliberation

    • The agent acts from character (ἕξις), not calculation

    • Death is clearly possible or likely

Crucially, Aristotle distinguishes true courage from:

  • Trained reflex (soldiers acting by drill)

  • Rashness

  • Fearlessness due to ignorance

The highest courage is shown “in sudden dangers” (ἐν τοῖς ἐξαίφνης κινδύνοις).

This directly captures the instinctive, unrehearsed element you’re emphasizing.

2. Greek: καλοκἀγαθία (kalokagathía) — “noble goodness revealed in action”

Less a single act than a moral unveiling.

  • Literally: the beautiful and the good

  • Used to describe moments where virtue becomes visible

  • Often applied retrospectively to heroic deaths

Your examples (Flight 93, Librescu, grenade cases) are textbook instances where:

Character, previously unknown, is suddenly made manifest.

In Greek thought, such acts are not decisions so much as revelations of what one already is.

3. Latin: virtus (in extremis) — “virtue under mortal pressure”

The Roman term virtus (from vir, “man”) originally meant:

  • Martial courage

  • Moral excellence proven by action

Romans explicitly valued improvised heroism:

  • Virtus non verbis sed factis — virtue shown in deeds, not words

In Livy and Tacitus, the purest virtus appears:

  • In last stands

  • In shield-over-comrade moments

  • In acts undertaken without expectation of survival

Your soldier-on-a-grenade example is paradigmatic virtus.

4. Latin: pietas (transcended) — duty overriding self-preservation

Less obvious, but important.

  • Pietas = devotion to family, gods, and community

  • In extreme moments, pietas can override the instinct to live

Liviu Librescu’s act fits especially well here:

  • Not courage for its own sake

  • But devotion to others as morally prior to self

Vergil’s Aeneas embodies pietas; later Roman moralists praised those who die fulfilling it.

5. Classical Chinese: 義 (yì) — righteous action without calculation

This is the best non-Western analogue.

In Confucian and Mencian thought:

  • 義 is doing what is right because it is right

  • It is opposed to 利 (lì), profit or advantage

  • In emergencies, 義 acts spontaneously

Mencius gives the famous example:

Seeing a child about to fall into a well, anyone would act immediately—not to gain praise, but from an unthinking moral impulse.

That unthinking impulse is 義 grounded in 仁 (rén), humaneness.

Flight 93 is 義 at scale.

6. Classical Chinese: 捨身取義 (shě shēn qǔ yì) — “to give up the body to take righteousness”

This phrase is nearly exact.

  • Literally: “abandon the body to preserve righteousness”

  • Used for:

    • Martyrs

    • Loyal officials

    • Sudden self-sacrifice

It explicitly frames death as secondary to moral action—and crucially, not deliberative.

7. Greek tragic vocabulary: εὐκλεὴς θάνατος (eukleēs thanatos) — a “noble death”

This describes the outcome, not the psychology, but matters culturally.

  • A death that gives meaning to a life

  • Often unexpected, unrehearsed

  • Recognized after the fact

Greek tragedy assumes that such deaths arise from character meeting circumstance.

Putting it together

If you wanted a single classical label, depending on tradition:

  • Greek (ethical precision): ἀνδρεία in sudden danger (andreía en tois exaiphnēs kindýnois)

  • Roman (moral gravitas): virtus in extremis

  • Chinese (closest semantic match): 捨身取義 (shě shēn qǔ yì)

Philosophically, all three agree on this core idea:

True virtue appears when there is no time to think, no benefit to gain, and no certainty of survival.
